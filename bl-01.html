What the gap looks like in practice

These aren't hypotheticals. They're organizational failures with named consequences.

Operational failure

Amazon's AI agent caused a 13-hour cloud outage

An AI agent called Kiro, deployed inside Amazon Web Services, autonomously chose to delete and recreate part of its environment, causing a 13-hour service interruption. Amazon called it "user error, not AI error." Security researchers disagreed: the AI had no visibility into customer impact, cost of downtime, or broader consequences of its actions. The real failure was that no one configured the guardrails before giving the AI autonomy.

13 hours of downtime — no human approved the action
The AI speed tax

48% of AI-first organizations say AI was directly exploited in their last incident

A global survey of 2,000 IT decision-makers found that AI-first organizations are far more likely to have AI play a direct role in their security incidents: 48% vs. just 10% for non-AI-first peers. AI systems expand the attack surface through agentic workflows and decentralized data flows. Fastly's CISO Marshall Erwin: "The priority isn't to slow down innovation. It's to modernise security at the same rate that AI is transforming infrastructure."

48% exploited via AI — vs. 10% for non-AI-first peers

Governance starts with named owners

Every gap in your AI security has a person who should own closing it. Here's where accountability lives.

🎯
You (Executive leadership)

Set the policy and mandate training

AI usage policy doesn't write itself. You establish what's acceptable, require training before tool access, and make security culture an organizational priority, not an IT footnote.

🛡
Security team / CISO

Evaluate tools and define access boundaries

Every AI tool needs a security evaluation before deployment. The security team defines what data AI tools can access, what they can't, and how to detect when those boundaries are violated.

💻
Engineering leadership

Require security review for AI-generated code

If your developers use AI coding tools, every code change needs security scanning. Engineering leadership mandates the review process and ensures it's followed. Not optional, not aspirational.

📚
HR / People operations

Deliver AI security training to every employee

Only 22% of organizations have enacted policies for AI training. HR owns the training mandate: what employees need to know before they use AI tools, refreshed as the tools evolve.
