Article 4 — the positive one

What safe AI use actually looks like

It starts with you. Not with tools, not with settings. With how you think.

Start with your mindset

It starts with a security mindset

Before settings, before tools, before any checklist, the most powerful thing you can build is a way of thinking. Not paranoia, curiosity. Not expertise, awareness. These three habits separate people who get caught off guard from people who don't.

🤔
Question things
When something feels off, like an unexpected link, a weird request, or an action you didn't ask for, don't ignore that feeling. Ask why. The AI won't be offended.
"Why did the AI add that link? I didn't ask for it."
"Why does it need access to my entire drive for one document?"
🔍
Explore what it does
You don't need to understand how AI works. But paying attention to what it's doing, what it's reading, what it's sending, what permissions it has, keeps you ahead of problems.
"What did the AI just do with my file?"
"Where is this information being stored?"
🛡
Prepare for when things go wrong
Don't assume everything will work perfectly. Assume that at some point, something will fail. Set yourself up so that when it does, the damage is small and fixable.
"If this conversation leaked, would I be okay?"
"If I accidentally shared this, could I undo it?"

Your 5 personal safety rules

Tap each rule to commit to it. These are yours to keep.

01
Set it and forget it
Configure safe defaults once. Let the settings protect you so you don't have to think about it every time.
02
Give the minimum
Only share what the AI needs for this specific task. Everything extra is unnecessary risk.
03
Pause on "send" and "access"
When the AI wants to do something, not just read, take two seconds to decide if it should.
04
Scan before you share
Glance at the output before it leaves your screen. Look for anything you didn't ask for.
05
When in doubt, ask
There are no stupid questions. Ask a colleague, ask IT, ask the AI itself to explain what it's doing. Asking is free.

You don't need to be an expert.

You need a security mindset: question things, explore what AI does, and prepare for when things go wrong. Combine that with five simple habits, and you're safer than most.
