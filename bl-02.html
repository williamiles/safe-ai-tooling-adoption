Article 2

You introduced AI to move fast.
Now make it safe too.

Speed and security aren't opposites. But without deliberate action, your organization is optimizing for one and exposed on the other.

Two groups in your organization right now

They're both moving fast with AI. Neither has what they need to do it safely. Tap the tabs to see their reality.

👤
Your non-technical employees
Exploring powerful new tools
What they're doing
What they don't realize
What they need from you
They paste client emails into AI tools to draft replies. They upload spreadsheets to get summaries. They share documents to brainstorm. They're discovering what AI can build, and they're not technical enough to know what they're exposing. They don't understand how permissions, credentials, and data sharing work on their machines.
Every document they share with an AI tool could contain sensitive data that leaves your organization's control. Hidden instructions embedded in files they receive can manipulate the AI into disclosing confidential information, without the employee noticing anything unusual. One manipulated document can turn an AI assistant into a data disclosure channel.
Clear guidelines on what data is acceptable to share with AI tools. Training that explains the risk in terms they understand, not technical jargon, but real consequences. And a safe channel to ask questions without feeling judged for using the tools. This is a training mandate and a policy decision. Both are in your lane.
💻
Your development team
Building faster than ever
What they're doing
What they don't realize
What they need from you
They use AI coding assistants to write code, review pull requests, and debug problems. Many run these tools in permissive modes that auto-approve actions to stay productive. They're more productive than ever, while performing risky operations on critical systems. They want to move fast. They're accepting permissions they haven't fully reviewed.
Research shows 87% of AI-generated code changes contain security flaws: authentication bypasses, access control failures, session management gaps. These are decade-old vulnerability classes being generated at scale. Pattern-based scanning tools miss the majority because they're logic flaws, not known-bad code. Your developers are shipping faster. The code quality isn't keeping up.
Mandatory security review for every AI-generated code change. Engineering leadership that treats AI-assisted development as a new workflow requiring new safeguards, not the same process with a faster keyboard. And budget for contextual security tooling that catches logic flaws, not just known patterns. This is a process mandate and a budget decision. Both are in your lane.

Your role is the environment.

You don't need to understand the technical details. You need to create the conditions where both groups can work productively and safely.

Your non-technical employees need policy and training.
Your developers need process and tooling.

Both need you to make security a priority, not an afterthought.

What "both speed and security" looks like

Three organizational shifts that don't slow your teams down. They protect what your teams are building.

📜

Guidelines instead of guesswork

Your employees are making security decisions every day, they just don't know it. A clear AI usage policy removes the guesswork: what data stays internal, what tools are approved, when to escalate. People move faster when the boundaries are visible.

🔍

Review built into the workflow

Security review shouldn't be a gate at the end. It should be woven into how your teams already work. For developers, that means scanning every AI-generated code change as part of the normal process, not as an extra step they'll skip under deadline pressure.

💬

A culture where everyone has a security mindset

You can't be in every room where an AI decision is made. But you can build a culture where every employee asks themselves: what could go wrong, and how do we minimize the consequences? When that habit is embedded, in how people use tools, share data, and approve actions, you've turned every employee into a security sensor. That scales. Your availability doesn't.

Actions you can take this week

Each one creates a safer environment for one of the two groups. Tap to commit.

Publish clear guidelines on what data employees can and cannot share with AI tools
Require a short AI security briefing before employees get access to new AI tools
Ask engineering leadership how AI-generated code is reviewed today, and what gaps exist
Budget for security tooling that catches logic flaws in AI-generated code
Create a no-blame reporting channel for AI-related security concerns
