Article 3

Governance questions every leader needs to answer.

Before you deploy AI further, you need a framework for how much autonomy to grant, and who is accountable at each level.

See the framework

How much autonomy does your AI have?

AI tools operate at different levels of autonomy. Each level carries different risks and requires different governance. Tap a level to explore.

Level 1 — Assistive AI

The AI suggests, the human decides. Every action requires explicit approval. This is where most organizations start: employees using ChatGPT, Copilot, or Claude to draft emails, summarize documents, and brainstorm ideas.
In your organization

Employee pastes a client email into an AI tool to draft a reply. AI suggests text; the employee reviews and sends it.

Organizational risk

Sensitive data leaves the organization when employees share it with AI tools. No visibility into what's being shared or with which tools.

Governance owner: You set the acceptable use policy. HR delivers the training. CISO defines which tools are approved.

Level 2 — Semi-Autonomous AI

The AI takes some actions on its own but asks for approval on sensitive operations. This is where many development teams are today: AI coding assistants that write and modify code, with the developer reviewing changes before they ship.
In your organization

A developer uses an AI assistant to write a login system. The AI generates code and modifies files; the developer reviews the pull request.

Organizational risk

77% of organizations lack the AI and data security practices to defend their pipelines and infrastructure. Approval fatigue leads developers to rubber-stamp changes under deadline pressure.

Governance owner: Engineering leadership mandates security review. CISO provides scanning tooling. You allocate the budget.

Level 3 — Autonomous AI Agents

The AI plans and executes multi-step tasks with minimal supervision. It accesses internal systems, reads private data, and takes actions across tools. This is where the risk escalates sharply: the AI is making decisions with organizational consequences.
In your organization

An AI agent monitors support tickets, reads customer data, drafts responses, and sends them, with only periodic human review.

Organizational risk

An agent with access to private data, untrusted content, and external communication can be manipulated into disclosing sensitive information without anyone noticing.

Governance owner: CISO defines access boundaries and monitoring. Engineering builds containment controls. You approve the risk level and sign off on deployment.

Level 4 — Fully Autonomous Systems

The AI operates independently across critical systems with no human in the loop. It makes decisions, takes actions, and handles consequences on its own. Very few organizations should be here today. The threat modeling required is exponentially more complex.
In your organization

An AI system manages infrastructure, deploys code, and responds to incidents autonomously, making production decisions without human approval.

Organizational risk

A single manipulation, data poisoning event, or logic error can cascade through production systems before any human is aware. Recovery costs scale with autonomy.

Governance owner: This requires board-level risk acceptance. Dedicated security oversight, continuous monitoring, and a tested rollback strategy are non-negotiable.

Seven questions you need to answer

If you can't answer these for your organization, you have governance gaps. Tap each to see what a good answer looks like.

1
Which AI tools are our employees using today? +
You need a complete inventory, not just officially purchased tools, but anything employees have signed up for individually. Shadow AI is your biggest blind spot. If you don't know what tools are in use, you can't govern them.
Owner: CISO
2
What data are those tools allowed to access? +
Define data classification tiers for AI usage. Public data - fine. Internal documents - with approved tools only. Client data, credentials, financial records - never shared with AI tools unless the tool has been security-evaluated and the data flow is controlled.
Owner: CISO + Legal
3
Who is accountable when an AI tool causes a data breach? +
"The AI did it" is not an accountability statement. You need a named human owner for every AI deployment. At Level 1–2, the user and their manager share responsibility. At Level 3–4, the person who approved the deployment is accountable.
Owner: You
4
How are we reviewing AI-generated outputs before they ship? +
For code: every AI-generated change scanned before merge. For content: human review before external publication. For automated actions: audit logs and periodic review. The review process must be mandatory, not optional, and it must match the autonomy level.
Owner: Engineering lead + CISO
5
Do we have an incident response plan that covers AI? +
Most IR plans predate AI deployment. You need AI-specific scenarios: manipulated AI disclosing data, poisoned AI outputs entering production, employees unknowingly sharing credentials through AI tools. If your IR plan doesn't cover these, it has a gap.
Owner: CISO
6
What autonomy level have we approved, and who signed off? +
Each autonomy level should be a deliberate decision with a documented sign-off, not a gradual drift. If your teams have moved from Level 1 to Level 3 without explicit governance review, you've accepted risk you haven't evaluated.
Owner: You + Board
7
How will we know when something goes wrong? +
Monitoring and detection: not just for traditional threats, but for AI-specific risks: unusual data flows from AI tools, unexpected external communications, AI outputs that deviate from expected behavior. If you can't detect it, you can't respond to it.
Owner: CISO + Engineering
