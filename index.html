We started using these AI tools because they're genuinely helpful — but nobody sat us down and explained what to watch out for.
You're smart for exploring AI. These tools help you draft emails, summarize documents, brainstorm ideas, and get things done faster than ever. That curiosity and willingness to learn new tools is exactly what makes you effective. But these tools can also read your files, access your accounts, and act on your behalf — and the line between helpful and risky isn't always visible.
You don't need to become technical to stay safe. You need a security mindset. That means building a habit of asking "what could go wrong?" before you act — and taking small steps to limit the damage if something does. A few specific things can go wrong with AI, and once you know what they are, you'll spot them without slowing down.
The shift isn't about learning to fear these tools — it's about moving from "this works, so it's fine" to "this works — but what did I just give it access to?"

We adopted AI because it's critical to how we compete — but the rollout moved fast and we're still catching up on what it means for risk.
AI adoption isn't optional — it's essential to how every organization evolves. You made the right call. But the introduction was fast, and in most organizations it happened before the risks were fully understood or the workforce was trained. Your employees are exploring powerful capabilities without guidance. Your developers are more productive than ever but operating in ways your existing security policies weren't designed for.
The question isn't whether to use AI — it's whether you've built the security mindset and culture to use it responsibly. That starts with understanding your exposure, educating your workforce, establishing clear ownership, and putting governance in place that doesn't kill the speed advantage. You bear the responsibility for how this rolls out.
The shift isn't about slowing down adoption — it's about moving from "we deployed it" to "we deployed it with a security strategy, trained our people, and know who owns each risk."

AI tools have made us faster and broader than we've ever been — but we're accepting permissions we haven't reviewed, running agents in permissive modes, and trusting output we haven't verified.
AI tools amplify your skills and your productivity. You can work in domains and codebases you have no former knowledge about. But AI tools have two blind spots you need to know. They write vulnerable code — repeating security mistakes that have existed for decades. And they have access to everything you do — your credentials, your environment, your production systems. One is a code quality problem. The other is a blast radius problem. Both are yours to manage.
You need a security mindset matched to the speed you're now operating at. That means knowing how to configure your AI tooling with safe defaults, leveraging the built-in security settings most developers skip, understanding what the AI will miss, and taking responsibility for the code you ship — regardless of who wrote the first draft.
The shift isn't about giving up productivity — it's about moving from "the AI wrote it and tests pass" to "I've verified the output, limited the blast radius, and locked down what this agent can reach."
